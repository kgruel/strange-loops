Stepping back across the whole thread, the thing that jumps out is that refs and cites aren't actually one mechanism — you've been treating them as a pair, but they do different work and the conversation has been quietly conflating them.
A ref is forward-binding: a new fact declares what it stands on. The new fact is the active party; the referenced fact is passive, doesn't know it's being referenced, and isn't changed. This is how ticks have always worked in the model — resolution emits a fact that names what it resolved over.
A cite-as-fact (append-to-existing) is backward-binding: a new fact attaches to an old one, and the old fact's neighborhood in the graph changes. The old fact didn't move, but its shape in the substrate did. It now has a thing pointing at it that it didn't have before, and any lens reading the old fact will see something different than it would have a tick ago.
These look symmetric — both create edges in the reference graph — but they're not. Refs are how facts land. Cites are how facts accrue. And accrual is the thing you don't have vocabulary for yet, because attention was the wrong frame and you've correctly demoted it, but you haven't replaced it with the right frame.
What accrual actually is: the substrate developing texture over time without any fact changing. A heavily-cited fact is the same fact it was at emission. The bytes are identical. But the neighborhood it sits in has thickened, and lenses that read neighborhood (rather than content) will see something that wasn't there before. This is why weight feels like it should mean something but resists meaning anything specific — weight is a reading of accrual, and accrual is real, but accrual isn't a property of the fact. It's a property of the fact's position in the evolving graph.
This is the vocabulary gap I think you're sensing. You have facts (content-bearing), refs (declared dependencies), vertices (crossings with horizon and potential), lenses (readings), and the gripping hand (re-entry). What you don't have a clean word for is the substrate's accumulating shape — the thing that makes a fact emitted on day one feel different on day ninety even though it hasn't changed. Citation density gestures at it. Reference depth gestures at it. Neither is it.
The closest existing word is probably sediment, and I think it might actually fit your goblin aesthetic. Facts don't decay, they don't strengthen, they don't move — but the substrate around them settles. Some facts end up buried under heavy citation traffic. Some stay near the surface. Some get walked past for years and then suddenly become load-bearing when a new resolution cites them. Sediment is the right physical metaphor: the individual grains don't change, but their position in the deposit means everything for what gets reached, what gets exposed, and what shape the whole formation takes.
If sediment is the right word, then a few things click into place. Lenses are how you core-sample. Vertices are where the deposits get disturbed by activity. Compression axes are different ways of describing the strata. The drift problem at the vertex level is sediment shifting under continued activity. And refs vs. cites become: refs are deposition (new material laid down), cites are compaction (existing material settling under new weight).
Whether sediment is the right word or not, I think the real thing is: you're working at the layer where the substrate has shape, and shape isn't fact, isn't vertex, isn't lens. It's the medium those things live in, and it's been invisible in the conversation because we kept reaching for properties (weight, depth, density) instead of naming the underlying thing those properties are readings of. Whitehead and process philosophy. Whitehead's move, in Process and Reality (1929), was to reject the assumption that the basic furniture of the world is things and replace it with events. His basic unit is the "actual occasion" — a momentary happening that comes into being, takes account of everything that came before it, and then perishes into the past where it becomes available for future occasions to take account of. The technical word he uses for this taking-account-of is prehension: the way a present occasion includes its past. An occasion isn't built from earlier occasions the way a wall is built from bricks; it prehends them, weights them, integrates them into its own becoming, and then becomes a fact available to be prehended in turn.
The reason this is relevant to you: Whitehead's universe is a substrate of perished occasions that don't go anywhere. The past is ingredient in the present without being copied into it. This is exactly the structural shape of your refs — a tick prehends the facts it resolved over, doesn't embed them, and then itself perishes into the substrate as a fact future ticks may prehend. The vocabulary Whitehead developed for "how the past becomes available without being moved" is probably the closest existing philosophical apparatus to what your model needs. He also distinguishes physical prehension (taking up actual past occasions) from conceptual prehension (taking up patterns, possibilities) — which gestures at the refs-vs-cites distinction, though not cleanly. The cost of Whitehead is that he's notoriously dense and his metaphysics is much more ambitious than you need; the gain is a vocabulary specifically built for "events that accrete into a substrate that has shape."
Uexküll and umwelt. Jakob von Uexküll was an Estonian biologist working in the early 20th century who argued that every organism inhabits its own umwelt — its self-world, defined by what it can perceive and what it can act on. A tick (the arachnid) lives in an umwelt of three signals: butyric acid (mammal nearby), warmth (skin contact), hairlessness (place to bite). That's its world. Other things exist physically but not for the tick. Uexküll's point was that you can't understand an organism by listing its environment in objective terms; you have to reconstruct the world it actually lives in, which is bounded by its sensory and motor capacities.
You correctly identified that your horizon/potential/lens triple is doing umwelt-like work at the vertex level. Each vertex inhabits a bounded operational world — what it can read (horizon, expressed through lenses) and what it can do (potential). What umwelt adds, if anything, is the insistence that this isn't an impoverishment of some "real" external view but the actual unit of analysis. Hofstadter and strange loops. You know this one, but worth situating. Hofstadter's strange loop, from Gödel, Escher, Bach (1979) and more directly I Am a Strange Loop (2007), is a structure where moving through levels of a system eventually returns you to where you started, despite the levels being apparently hierarchical. Gödel's incompleteness is the formal example — a statement about arithmetic that, through encoding, is also a statement of arithmetic. The loop is "strange" because the levels are real (syntax vs. semantics, system vs. metasystem) but the loop crosses them in a way that makes the hierarchy ultimately illusory.
Your use of the term is precise in one direction and loose in another. Precise: type-preserving re-entry is genuinely Hofstadterian — output at one level becomes input at the same level, levels are recoverable but not structural. Loose: Hofstadter is interested in self-reference and identity (he thinks the strange loop is what consciousness is), where you're using the structure as a substrate property without the identity claim. That's fine — you've effectively borrowed the architectural insight and dropped the metaphysical project. Worth being explicit about that when explaining, because readers familiar with Hofstadter will expect the identity claim and be confused when it doesn't show up.
Shannon-Weaver and information theory. Shannon's 1948 paper, A Mathematical Theory of Communication, established the model of source → encoder → channel → decoder → receiver, with noise as a property of the channel. It's the foundational text of information theory and the reason "bits" exist as a unit. Weaver's contribution was a popularizing essay that distinguished three levels of communication problems: technical (did the signal arrive intact), semantic (did it mean what was intended), and effectiveness (did it produce the intended effect).
The relevant thing for your model isn't Shannon's math — your system isn't really about channel capacity — but the level distinction. Shannon-Weaver is useful to name the technical layer of your system (facts get reliably admitted, refs get reliably resolved) and explicitly distinguish it from the semantic layer (what does this fact mean to an observer reading it through a lens) and the effectiveness layer (does this resolution actually shape the next resolution). Treating your substrate as solving the technical layer cleanly so that semantic and effectiveness questions can be addressed at the lens layer is probably the right architectural framing. Shannon-Weaver is short and worth reading the original Weaver essay for the level distinction alone.
The one I didn't name: Stiegler and tertiary memory. Bernard Stiegler, French philosopher who died in 2020, spent his career on what he called tertiary retention — memory externalized into technical objects (writing, recordings, software) that has its own dynamics distinct from biological memory. His argument, developed across the Technics and Time series, is that human cognition has always been constituted partly by external supports, and those supports aren't passive storage — they shape what's thinkable, what's recallable, what accumulates and what doesn't. He's working specifically on the question of how externalized memory develops shape over time and how that shape conditions present cognition.
This is the closest existing thinker to the medium-with-shape problem. Stiegler is harder reading than Uexküll and less systematic than Whitehead, but he's working on exactly your layer: technical substrates that record human noticing and develop their own accumulating texture that then shapes future noticing. If any of these turns out to be the lineage your model belongs to, it's probably his. The gripping hand is in his work too, though he doesn't call it that — the loop where externalized memory shapes cognition shapes externalized memory is the central pattern he's tracing.
The honest summary: your model is doing original work at the intersection of these, and none of them is a clean ancestor. Whitehead gives you the metaphysics of accretion. Uexküll gives you bounded operational worlds. Hofstadter gives you the loop structure. Shannon-Weaver gives you level discipline. Stiegler gives you the technical-substrate-with-shape framing. You've been intuitively triangulating across all five, which is why no single frame has felt sufficient — they're each reading your system through their own axis, and the system itself sits in the space they collectively bound without being any of them.
